# What the AI Bubble Means for the US Economy

*Analysis current as of February 2026*

---

## The US Economy Is Already Dependent on AI Spending

The US economy has become structurally reliant on AI capex continuing:

| Metric | Figure |
|---|---|
| Harvard economist Jason Furman's estimate | AI investment = **92% of US GDP growth** in H1 2025 |
| GDP growth without AI investment | **Just 0.1% annualised** — near standstill |
| More conservative St. Louis Fed estimate | AI = **20–25%** of real GDP growth |
| Total AI data centre build-out planned | **$3 trillion** |
| Hyperscaler bond issuance expected (2026) | **$250–300 billion** — record levels |
| US consumers actually spending on AI services | **$12 billion/year** — vs $500B+ in capex |

The gap between what's being **spent** ($500B+) and what's being **earned** ($12B from consumers) is the definition of an unsustainable bubble. The US economy has essentially been kept alive by companies spending money they don't yet have on infrastructure for customers who don't yet exist at the required scale.

---

## Two Simultaneous Crises — Not One

What makes this uniquely dangerous is that the US faces **two contradictory AI-driven economic shocks at the same time:**

```
SHOCK 1: AI bubble bursts          SHOCK 2: AI displaces jobs
─────────────────────────          ───────────────────────────
Capex collapses                    White-collar unemployment rises
Stock market crashes                Consumer spending falls
Wealth effect hits consumption     Tax revenues fall
Credit markets freeze               Social safety net strained
Construction/tech jobs lost         Middle-class squeeze
```

Normally a bubble burst causes unemployment which causes recession. Here, AI is **also destroying jobs at the same time the bubble is inflating** — meaning the safety valve of "workers find new jobs" is compromised before the crash even happens.

---

## The Wealth Effect: Why This Hits Consumers Hard

US households are **more exposed to equities than at any point in history:**

- In 2000 (dot-com peak): households held **~32%** of financial wealth in equities
- Today: **~40%** of financial wealth in equities
- S&P 500 concentration: top 5 companies = **30% of the index** (all AI-driven)

If the AI bubble pops like the dot-com bubble:
- Dot-com wiped out **~$6 trillion** (60% of 1990s GDP)
- An equivalent crash today would wipe out **~$33 trillion** — more than **entire current US GDP**
- Every American with a 401(k), pension, or savings account feels this

A 20% equity decline alone won't cause recession. But a **50%+ decline** — which dot-com-style corrections reach — almost certainly would.

---

## The Job Displacement Crisis Running in Parallel

Even before any bubble bursts, AI is already hollowing out white-collar employment:

| Category | Detail |
|---|---|
| Jobs directly cut citing AI (2025) | **55,000** — out of 1.17M total layoffs |
| White-collar jobs facing extinction (Microsoft data) | **5 million** |
| Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei's projection | AI eliminates **50% of entry-level white-collar jobs** within 5 years |
| Potential US unemployment rate (Amodei) | **10–20%** |
| Roles most at risk | Accounting, legal drafting, compliance, junior software, customer service |

The cruel irony: the AI that's supposed to generate the ROI to justify the infrastructure spend is simultaneously destroying the jobs of the people who would pay for the AI services.

---

## The Dot-Com Comparison — And Why This Could Be Worse

| Factor | Dot-Com 2000 | AI Bubble 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| Primary asset | Tech stocks (paper wealth) | Tech stocks + **physical infrastructure debt** |
| Household equity exposure | **32%** of financial wealth | **40%** of financial wealth |
| Value at risk | ~$6 trillion | **~$33 trillion** |
| Corporate debt involved | Modest | **$3 trillion data centre build-out** |
| Job market cushion | Strong pre-crash employment | Already seeing white-collar displacement |
| Geographic concentration | Silicon Valley + NASDAQ | Nationwide (data centres in every state) |
| Recovery mechanism | New industries absorbed workers | **AI is the new industry and it's not hiring** |

The dot-com bust was painful but the underlying economy was healthy — unemployment was low, consumer debt was manageable, and new industries (social media, smartphones) emerged to absorb workers. **None of those cushions exist this time.**

---

## The Credit Market Contagion Risk

The $3 trillion data centre build-out hasn't been funded from profits — it's been funded by debt:

- Hyperscalers expected to issue **$250–300B in bonds in 2026 alone** — record levels
- Oracle: **$108B+ in debt**
- CoreWeave and other AI infrastructure firms: heavily leveraged
- SoftBank: **$20B margin loans against ARM shares**

If the AI revenue doesn't materialise to service this debt:
- **Credit rating downgrades** cascade across the sector
- Bond prices fall — hurting pension funds, insurers, and banks holding this paper
- **Credit markets freeze** — exactly as they did in 2008, but triggered by AI debt rather than mortgage debt
- The transmission mechanism from "AI fails" to "financial crisis" runs through the bond market

Oliver Wyman's analysis calls this the **"hidden fault line"** — the debt structures are more dangerous than the equity valuations because debt default has systemic consequences that equity losses don't.

---

## The Geographic Dimension: Not Just Wall Street

Unlike the dot-com bust, which was concentrated in coastal tech hubs, the AI infrastructure buildout has spread across America:

- Data centres built in **Texas, Arizona, Virginia, Ohio, Georgia**
- Power grid infrastructure committed in every region
- Construction jobs, electricians, cooling engineers hired nationwide
- **Local governments have issued tax breaks and infrastructure bonds** to attract data centres

When the buildout slows or stops:
- Regional construction booms reverse
- Power companies are left with stranded grid upgrades
- Local government finances are hit as promised tax revenues don't materialise
- The pain is **nationally distributed**, not confined to Silicon Valley

## The Scenario Map

| Scenario | Probability | US Economic Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Soft landing — AI finds ROI, capex moderates | 20% | Mild slowdown, manageable correction |
| Gradual deflation — bubble slowly unwinds | 35% | 2–3 year stagnation, unemployment rises to 5–6%, moderate recession |
| Sharp correction — bubble pops in 2026–27 | 30% | Severe recession, 8–10% unemployment, $10–20T wealth destruction |
| Systemic crisis — debt contagion triggers credit freeze | 15% | Depression-level event, government intervention required |

---

## The Bitter Irony for America

The US made a strategic bet: dominate AI, dominate the 21st century economy. The bet required:

1. Massive private capital investment ✓
2. Government support (Stargate, CHIPS Act) ✓
3. AI actually delivering AGI-level productivity gains ✗ *(not yet)*
4. That productivity paying for the infrastructure ✗ *(not happening)*

The result is an economy that has **crowded out other investment** — manufacturing, clean energy, infrastructure — in favour of a single technology bet. That bet is looking increasingly shaky exactly when the bill comes due.

> *"A huge chunk of US GDP growth is being kept alive by AI spending with no guaranteed return."*
> — Deutsche Bank / Fortune, December 2025
